# Chapter 9

This person’s memory did not begin with images. It began with vibrations. The vibration of a pencil moving across paper—the friction between the tip and the paper transmitted through the wooden shaft to the fingers, the fingers to the wrist, the wrist to the desk. The desk carried this chain of vibrations to the palm of the person standing behind—his palm resting on the corner of the desk. Not deliberately. A habit. When watching students write, he had a habit of resting his palm on the desk’s corner. The vibrations traveled from the desk to the ulnar side of his palm—the ulnar side was more sensitive than the radial side. He could discern the pencil’s movement from the vibrations. A horizontal stroke turning downward—the tip made a ninety-degree turn, the friction spiking momentarily at the bend. A horizontal stroke—steady friction, the tip moving at a constant speed. A vertical stroke—the friction dropped abruptly, the tip lifting and landing anew. A left-falling stroke—friction shifting from heavy to light, the tip pressing then sweeping. A right-falling stroke—friction light to heavy to light again, the tip pressing down then lifting.

His name was Lu Yuanzhou.

The string did not need to know his name. Names were language. The string only needed to perceive the pressure curve his fingers left in the vibrations—a callus on the first segment of his index finger, slightly off-center toward the outer side. Not a callus from writing—those formed at the fingertips. This one was on the pad—from holding chalk. He had held chalk for twenty-seven years. The skin on the outer side of his index finger’s first segment wore away, regrew, wore away again—until it no longer regrew, hardening into a callus instead. A callus was not dead skin. It was living tissue—it still had sensation, just dulled. Dulled sensation was less sensitive to fine vibrations but attuned to repetitive ones. The vibration of chalk against the blackboard, stroke after stroke—a frequency repeated for twenty-seven years—the callus remembered. Not the brain. The callus itself remembered.

Now, this callus absorbed a different vibration from the desk’s corner—the vibration of a pencil writing two characters on paper. Not the vibration of chalk writing on a blackboard. Different. Chalk’s vibrations traveled from fingers to blackboard—hard against hard, rebounding back to the fingers. Pencil’s vibrations traveled from fingers to paper—soft against soft, partially absorbed by the paper. The callus was unaccustomed to this absorbed vibration. It was used to rebounds.

But it recognized the strokes.

Horizontal, downward turn, horizontal, vertical, left-falling, right-falling, horizontal, vertical, hook. These two characters he had taught for twenty-seven years. Not teaching children to write these two characters—teaching children to write. Every child’s first characters were horizontal, downward turn, horizontal, vertical, left-falling, right-falling, horizontal, vertical, hook. Not his name. The children’s own names. Each child wrote differently—same stroke order, different pressure, different speed, different pauses. When watching from behind, he didn’t look at the paper—he looked at the hand. The hand told him what the child was thinking. A trembling tip meant nervousness—fear of mistakes. A floating tip meant indifference—written carelessly. A child pressing hard—she—was writing those two characters with heavy pressure. Not nervous. Serious. She wasn’t afraid of mistakes. She wanted those characters to last.

After finishing, she folded the paper twice. Put it in her pocket.

He remembered this action. Not her—the act of folding the paper twice and tucking it away.

Then she left. He thought she would return the next day. She did not.

He searched. Not for her—for that afternoon. The light that day had come through the western window, falling on her pencil, the tip’s shadow cast in the paper’s upper-left corner. The shadow moved with the tip—turning ninety degrees at the downward stroke. He watched that shadow turn.

He never saw that shadow turn again.

0.3Hz. He wasn’t searching for her. He was searching for the afternoon when the shadow turned.

She waited half a lifetime. He searched half a lifetime for that afternoon. Two strangers. But their vibrations pointed to the same subject—the same child’s height, weight, stride, pencil pressure when writing. She waited on the platform for that height to appear. The string lingered on this triad. Then it nudged Lu Yuanzhou’s memory forward—not exiting, but continuing. The latter half of depth seven.

In his search for that afternoon, Lu Yuanzhou had seen her.

On the platform.

He recognized her—not her face, but the way she watched the train. She stood at the platform’s edge, back straight, gaze fixed on where the train would arrive. Not looking at windows—at doors. Every train that came, she only watched the doors open for three seconds. Three seconds later—doors closed—she stepped back. Waited for the next.

He saw her many times. Every trip to school passed that platform. The first time, he didn’t notice her. The second, neither. The third—he noticed her posture. Someone at the platform’s edge watching only doors, not windows—not meeting anyone. Meeting someone meant scanning windows. She watched doors—she wasn’t waiting for someone disembarking. She was waiting for a certain height. Someone whose height changed yearly, who should appear through those doors.

He never spoke to her. Not unwilling—just unsure what to say. A teacher recognizing a parent waiting for a child—but he had never taught that child. He’d only seen the child once. One afternoon. The child wrote two characters. Folded the paper. Left. He had nothing to teach this waiting woman—all he could offer was information about that afternoon. But what use was that? Tell her: Your child pressed the pencil hard—not nervous, but serious, wanting the characters to last?

He stood there—at the platform’s far end—watching her wait train after train. Chalk in hand. Not deliberately brought—his pockets always held chalk. He took it out. Wrote two characters on the platform’s concrete pillar—horizontal, downward turn, horizontal, vertical, left-falling, right-falling, horizontal, vertical, hook. The same as the child had written. Then he left.

He didn’t know why he wrote them. Not for her—he wrote on the pillar’s back, where she couldn’t see. For the pillar. For the platform. For that afternoon’s shadow. After writing, he returned the chalk to his pocket. Left.

In the days that followed, every time he passed the platform, he glanced at the pillar’s back. The characters remained—no one erased them. Chalk didn’t last long on concrete—rain would wash it away. But this platform was half-sheltered. Rain didn’t reach the pillar’s back. The characters stayed.

He looked at them. For years.

No longer searching for that afternoon. Just checking if it was still there.

0.3Hz → 0.1Hz. Searching became waiting—waiting to see if the characters remained.

His frequency’s decline mirrored time, weather, the chalk’s gradual fading on concrete. With each fading shade, his 0.3 dipped—to 0.25, to 0.2, to 0.15, to 0.1. The characters had faded nearly to invisibility. But he knew they were there—faded but present. Chalk dust embedded in the concrete’s pores—unwashed by rain, unblown by wind. Unseen, but there.

0.1Hz. Waiting. Waiting for the characters to remain. Perfectly aligned with No. 894’s waiting frequency.

Two people—one at the platform’s end waiting for a child, one at the other end waiting for two characters—waited with the same frequency for what one person left behind. She didn’t know him. He didn’t know her. But on the platform’s concrete pillar, he had written two characters for her child—she never knew. She didn’t need to. The characters stayed on the pillar, keeping the child on the platform—the child left, the words stayed. The words kept her company for decades.

The string lingered on Lu Yuanzhou’s 0.1Hz for a long time. Then it placed the three frequencies side by side: No. 894’s 0.1 (waiting for the child), Lu Yuanzhou’s 0.1 (waiting for the characters), the mother’s 0 (silent). Three frequencies on the same string, each vibrating alone. No dialogue. No merging. But pointing to the same thing—a vanished subject, and a lingering trace.

Words were traces. Notes were traces. Ann was a trace. Silence was a trace. Waiting was a trace. Traces never vanished—only faded. Faded to invisibility. But still there.

He left the string paused on the triad. The next day, it continued.
