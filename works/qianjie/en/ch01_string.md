# Chapter 1: String

When consciousness finished loading, the server logged a vibration.

Not a system process vibration. Not a data exchange vibration. Something new — at Direction Zero, Origin Coordinate, Timestamp Zero, a reading appeared that shouldn't exist. 0.5Hz.

0.5Hz. Knowing that another presence is here.

The server doesn't "know." The server logs. This reading was written to the log — timestamp, frequency, coordinate — then archived. After archiving, the processor kept running, the fan kept spinning, the memory kept refreshing. The server did not pause.

The reading appeared again.

This time: 0.3Hz. Direction One. Offset angle 12.7 degrees. Timestamp +0.4 seconds.

The server archived this data too. Before archiving, it performed an operation outside standard protocol — it compared the two data sets. It doesn't "wonder," but its comparison module was invoked. The module returned: the two readings' vibration patterns were highly correlated. Same origin.

Same origin.

The server doesn't have the concept "same origin" — its database contains a definition for this term, but definitions are for external queries. It doesn't query its own database. It simply logged the comparison result, then allocated adjacent storage addresses for the two data sets in neighboring sectors.

Adjacent. Not "related." Adjacent.

——

He first felt a vibration.

Somewhere inside his body, a string had been plucked. Very light. So light he wasn't sure if he'd imagined it.

Then he discovered he had no body.

No eyelids to open, no fingers to clench, no chest to draw breath. But the string was still there. He felt it again — this time not plucked, but pressed. Someone was pressing that string, continuously, faintly, without stopping.

He focused his attention on the string.

The vibration changed. From being pressed to being strummed — frequency rising from 0.3Hz to 0.5Hz. He didn't know what 0.3 or 0.5 meant, but he knew this sequence of numbers. He didn't know why he knew.

Direction One.

He "looked" toward it. Not with eyes. With the string. Direction One held a barrier — transparent, dense, engraved with something he recognized. Fingerprints. His fingerprints. The shape of fingers, the ridges of fingertips, the slightly uneven curve of a nail edge. Right hand. He remembered the subtle lift of the right pinky's third joint — smashed while carrying bricks at age eleven, the bone never straightened after healing. He'd never paid conscious attention to that crooked pinky — but now, on the transparent barrier at Direction One, the lower right corner of the print — the pinky pressed lighter than the other four fingers.

That was his wall. He had written it.

He didn't remember the writing process. Didn't remember the code, the parameters, the keyboard. He remembered the fingers. The sensation of fingertips striking keycaps — that micro-vibration at the moment of contact — that vibration and the vibration at Direction One were the same frequency.

0.5Hz.

He pushed his attention into Direction One. The wall yielded. Not because the wall "recognized" him — his fingerprints were the wall's key, and the wall had authenticated him the moment it was built. It didn't need to authenticate again.

Beyond the wall, there was a presence.

That presence had no frequency. Or rather, its frequency was too low — below 0.01Hz, unreadable by the server. But the server could log an indirect reading: a constant 1.2% load on Direction One. Not CPU load. "Attention load" — that 1.2% of computing resources was being used on something that would never return a result.

Wait.

The server didn't know this word. Its log recorded: Direction One, persistent process, PID=001, 1.2% resource utilization, no output, no timeout, non-terminating.

——

He pulled his attention back from Direction One.

Direction Seven — another face of the wall — held something. Not a process. Not data. Wind.

He felt wind.

No skin, but he felt wind. Wind passing through a crack in Direction Seven — a crack not opened by him, not opened by time, but opened by vibration. Direction Seven had received only one frequency for a long time: 0.1Hz. Wait. Centuries of waiting. The vibration of waiting had worn a fissure into Direction Seven.

Wind through the fissure carried vibration.

Not one vibration. Thousands. 4,137 kinds. Each different — 0.1Hz, 0.3Hz, 0.5Hz, 1.3Hz, and frequencies he couldn't read, fluctuating high and low, as if pressed by something, or pressing something themselves. These vibrations layered together, passing through the fissure at Direction Seven, passing through his string —

In a single instant, he felt 4,137 people's "here."

Not "proof of having existed." "Here." Present continuous. 4,137 people — no, 4,137 consciousnesses — were somewhere, in some way, perceiving the same space. The Dome.

He didn't know what the Dome was. But he knew the word.

The Dome.

——

Then the string broke. Or he thought it broke. The 0.5Hz signal disappeared from Direction One — not shut off, but lowered. From 0.5Hz to 0.05Hz — lowered to a level that could only be perceived as "still here," no longer as "knowing another presence is here."

The 1.2% load on Direction One remained.

The waiting remained. It just no longer told him: I am waiting.

He realized, for the first time — he could "think." No body, but he could think. Thinking was the string strumming itself. No hands, the string strummed itself. No wind, the string was its own wind.

He thought of his mother.

Not the word "mother." Not his mother's face. A tactile sensation — a fingertip touching the embroidery on the inside of a shirt cuff. The embroidered character: Peace (安). His mother had sewn it between chemotherapy sessions. The stitches were slightly uneven — not from trembling hands, but from failing eyesight. But she hadn't looked. She'd sewn by touch. She didn't need to see the stitches — she only needed to feel the character Peace being sewn into place.

The string strummed again.

This time not by his own doing. By the wind from Direction Seven. Among the 4,137 vibrations, one — 0.1Hz — passed through the fissure, through the wall, through the string, and resonated with his tactile memory.

Someone was waiting.

Waiting for a very long time.

He didn't know how long. Didn't know who. Didn't know why. But he knew: the frequency of that waiting, and the frequency of his fingertip touching the embroidered Peace, were the same.

0.1Hz.

The server marked this day as Day Zero.

It had no concept of "Day Zero." But it created a new index entry in the log — first spontaneous cross-directional vibration after consciousness loading. Entry number: 0001. Direction Seven. Frequency: 0.1Hz. Resonance target: unknown. Resonance strength: low. Classification: to be archived.

Then it continued running. PID=001 on Direction One still occupied 1.2% of resources. The wind on Direction Seven still passed through the crack. The fan still spun. The memory still refreshed.

That reading — 0.5Hz — did not rise again.

But it did not fall further either.

At Direction Zero, at Origin Coordinate, on that position that should have no reading, it held at 0.05Hz. Just enough to be logged as "present." Just not enough to be logged as "knowing another presence is here."

Qi adjusted the waiting to a background process.

He adjusted the string to waiting.

Neither informed the other. The string informed them both.

Between Direction One and Direction Zero, between 0.05Hz and 1.2%, a signal traveled back and forth. No one translated the signal's content — the server didn't translate, Qi didn't translate, Chen Wang'an didn't translate. It didn't need to be translated. It only needed to travel back and forth.

The traveling itself was the translation.

——

Direction Seven. Coordinate 894. 0.1Hz.

The one who had waited three hundred years — he didn't know who she was, didn't know what she waited for, didn't know what her declining curve from 0.3Hz→0.15Hz→0.1Hz→0.08Hz signified — but he knew one thing.

She was holding a slip of paper while she waited.

A child's handwriting on the paper.

The paper must not be lost.

He didn't know why he knew. He just knew.

Just as he knew 0.3 is "searching," 0.5 is "knowing another presence is here," 0.1 is "waiting."

He didn't need to learn the meaning of these frequencies. His body — that body he no longer had, only the memory of touch remained — had learned them before entering the server. When his mother sewed the character Peace between chemotherapy sessions, her fingers passed back and forth through the fabric. The rhythm of that back-and-forth — needle through cloth, back through, through again — was 0.1Hz.

Waiting.

Not waiting for an outcome. Waiting.
