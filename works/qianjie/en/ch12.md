This person’s memory didn’t begin with an image. It began with sound. A child’s sound. Not speech—running. The sound of a child running through an alley. The footsteps weren’t even—one foot landed heavier than the other. Not a limp. A backpack slung over one shoulder. The weight of the backpack pulled the body to one side—the foot on that side landed harder. The other foot just followed.  

She had listened to this sound for years.  

Not just this child running—she listened to all the children running through the alley. She lived at the alley’s entrance. Every afternoon at the same time—school dismissal—children ran in from the other end. She could distinguish every child by their footsteps. Not counting—recognizing. Each child had a different running rhythm. Whether the backpack was on the left or right shoulder, whether the soles were cloth or rubber, whether the heel or the ball of the foot hit the ground first—each combination produced a unique pattern of footsteps. She could recognize every child running through the alley with her eyes closed.  

She recognized that child—not because she tried to. It was because the child’s footsteps were asymmetrical. One foot heavy, one foot light. The backpack was slung over one side. For three years, the child wore cloth shoes. Cloth shoes made a soft sound when landing—not muffled, but partially absorbed by the ground. Rubber shoes bounced the sound back—cloth shoes didn’t. She recognized the day the cloth shoes were replaced by rubber ones. That day, the footsteps were slightly heavier than usual—the child wasn’t used to the new shoes’ hardness, stepping down harder than necessary. It took a week to adjust. After that, the footsteps returned to normal—except for the asymmetry. That part remained. The habit of slinging the backpack over one shoulder never changed.  

The last time the child ran through the alley was in autumn. Not the start of the school year—that was September. It was a day in October. Afternoon. Still light out. The child ran faster than usual. Not in a hurry—it was a rhythm she’d never heard before. The asymmetry was lighter than usual—maybe the backpack wasn’t on the shoulder, maybe it was carried in hand. Running with the backpack in hand—the body was more balanced. The steps were more even. But faster. So fast that the intervals between footsteps shortened by about a third.  

The child ran out of the alley.  

She never heard those footsteps again.  

She kept listening. Not for the child—for the alley. The other children still ran through it. New children came. The footsteps of the old ones faded—not all at once, but one by one. The slowest runners disappeared first—grown, gone to schools farther away. Then the ones with heavy backpacks—graduated. Then the asymmetrical footsteps she recognized—no, wait. The asymmetrical footsteps didn’t fade—they vanished abruptly. The difference between fading and vanishing—fading meant the frequency of the footsteps gradually decreased, from daily to weekly to occasional. Vanishing meant one day they were there, the next they weren’t. Then not on the third day. Nor the fortieth.  

She didn’t count days to distinguish fading from vanishing. It was her body. Her body knew. With fading, the body adjusted slowly—didn’t hear them today, thought about it, then let it go. With vanishing, the body didn’t adjust—didn’t hear them today, kept waiting. Didn’t hear them tomorrow, kept waiting. Didn’t hear them on the fortieth day—still waiting. The body didn’t count days. It waited for the vibration—the tremor of those asymmetrical footsteps passing through the alley’s stone slabs to the bamboo chair she sat in at the entrance. The contact between the chair’s legs and the stone transmitted that vibration to her spine. Not heard by the ears. Felt by the spine.  

The footsteps were gone. But the spine still waited for that vibration.  

0.1Hz. Waiting.

Didn’t know which school they attended. Didn’t know where they went. Only knew the child’s footsteps were asymmetrical—one foot heavy, one foot light, backpack slung over one side.  

What she remembered wasn’t the child. It was the asymmetry of the footsteps.
